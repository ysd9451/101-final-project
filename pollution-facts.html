PLASTIC POLLUTION FACTS

Understanding the scale and impact of plastic pollution on our planet and health

Plastic in Our Oceans

Environmental

Every year, millions of tons of plastic waste enter our oceans, harming marine life and ecosystems. This plastic breaks down into microplastics, which are then ingested by marine animals and can enter the human food chain.

Source: Pew Charitable Trusts & SYSTEMIQ, 2020

5
grams

Microplastics in Humans

Health

Studies have found microplastic particles in human blood, lungs, and even placentas, indicating widespread exposure. The long-term health effects are still being researched, but it raises concerns about chronic inflammation and toxicity.

Source: WWF, 2019 (average weekly intake)

450
years

Plastic Decomposition Time

Environmental

Unlike organic materials, most plastics do not biodegrade. A single plastic bottle can take hundreds of years to break down into smaller pieces, persisting in the environment for generations.

Source: NOAA Marine Debris Program

9
percent

Global Plastic Recycling Rate

Environmental

Globally, only a small fraction of plastic waste is actually recycled. The vast majority ends up in landfills, incinerators, or pollutes natural environments, highlighting the inefficiency of current waste management systems.

Source: National Geographic, 2018

700
species

Marine Species Affected

Environmental

Plastic pollution impacts a wide range of marine species, from tiny plankton to large whales. Animals can become entangled in plastic debris or ingest it, leading to injury, starvation, and death.

Source: UN Environment Programme

380
million tons

Rising Plastic Production

Environmental

Plastic production has surged dramatically over the past decades and continues to rise. This increasing production outpaces efforts to manage waste, exacerbating the global pollution crisis.

Source: Our World in Data, 2023

10000
chemicals

Chemicals in Plastics

Health

Many plastics contain harmful chemicals like phthalates and BPA, which can leach into food and water. These chemicals are endocrine disruptors and have been linked to various health issues.

Source: UNEP, 2023 (chemicals in plastics)

40
percent

Single-Use Plastic Dominance

Environmental

Single-use plastics, such as packaging, bottles, and bags, constitute a significant portion of plastic waste. Their short lifespan and high volume contribute disproportionately to pollution.

Source: Plastic Waste Makers Index, 2021
